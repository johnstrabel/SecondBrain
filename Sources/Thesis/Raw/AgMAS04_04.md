# Park & Irwin (2004) — The Profitability of Technical Analysis: A Review

**Full citation:** Park, C.H. & Irwin, S.H. (2004). The Profitability of Technical Analysis: A Review. AgMAS Project Research Report 2004-04, University of Illinois.

## Summary

Comprehensive review of the evidence on technical analysis profitability. Covers survey studies (30–40% of practitioners use TA at short horizons), theoretical models (EMH rules out TA profits; behavioral/noisy rational expectation models allow it), and empirical studies. Categorizes technical trading systems: dual moving average crossover, channel breakout, relative strength, Alexander's filter rule. Surveys early empirical studies (1960–1987) and later studies.

## Key Arguments

1. **Practitioner use:** Survey evidence — 30–40% of practitioners believe TA is important for price determination at horizons up to 6 months.
2. **Theoretical context:** Classical EMH (martingale/random walk) rules out TA profits; noisy rational expectation and behavioral models allow TA to work by exploiting noise trader and feedback effects.
3. **Four major TA systems:** (1) dual MA crossover, (2) outside price channel, (3) RSI, (4) Alexander's filter rule — all have been studied.
4. **Early empirical studies (1960–87):** Generally negative evidence; Brock et al. (1992) was the first convincing positive evidence.
5. **Later studies:** Mixed; data-snooping concerns (White 1997, Sullivan et al. 1999) cloud positive results.

## Relevance to Thesis

- Provides the literature foundation for the technical analysis pillar of the hierarchical information fusion framework
- Motivates including technical/price-based features in the ML model (price/volume dominate: 30% + 13.2%)
- Cites Brock et al. and Sullivan et al. as the core positive/negative evidence debate
