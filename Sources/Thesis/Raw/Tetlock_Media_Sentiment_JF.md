# Tetlock (2007) — Giving Content to Investor Sentiment: The Role of Media in the Stock Market

**Full citation:** Tetlock, P.C. (2007). Giving Content to Investor Sentiment: The Role of Media in the Stock Market. *Journal of Finance*, 62(3), 1139–1168.

## Summary

Quantitatively measures media–market interactions using daily content from the WSJ "Abreast of the Market" column (1984–1999). Finds that high media pessimism predicts downward price pressure followed by reversion to fundamentals, and unusually high or low pessimism predicts high trading volume. Results are consistent with noise/liquidity trader models.

## Key Arguments

1. **Method:** General Inquirer (GI) content analysis applied to WSJ column; PCA of 77 GI categories yields a "pessimism" factor.
2. **Main finding:** High pessimism → next-day market decline, then reversal — consistent with noise trading, not information transmission.
3. **Volume:** Extreme pessimism (in either direction) → high trading volume; supports liquidity trader models.
4. **Rules out alternatives:** Evidence inconsistent with (a) media as proxy for fundamental news, (b) media as proxy for volatility, (c) media as sideshow with no market relationship.
5. **Implications:** Media sentiment is a measurable, tradeable investor sentiment signal.

## Key Quotes

> "High media pessimism predicts downward pressure on market prices followed by a reversion to fundamentals."

> "Unusually high or low pessimism predicts high market trading volume."

> "The evidence is inconsistent with theories of media content as a proxy for new information about fundamental asset values."

## Relevance to Thesis

- Foundational precedent for using text-based sentiment as a predictor of market outcomes
