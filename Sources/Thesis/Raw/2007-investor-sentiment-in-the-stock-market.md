# Baker & Wurgler (2007) — Investor Sentiment in the Stock Market

**Full citation:** Baker, M. & Wurgler, J. (2007). Investor Sentiment in the Stock Market. *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 21(2), 129–151.

## Summary

Review article on how investor sentiment affects stock prices. Two foundational assumptions: (1) DeLong et al. (1990) noise trader model — investors subject to sentiment; (2) Shleifer & Vishny (1997) — limits to arbitrage. The question is no longer *whether* sentiment affects prices but *how* to measure it and *which stocks* are most affected.

## Key Arguments

1. **Noise traders + limited arbitrage:** Sentiment-driven mispricing can persist when arbitrage is risky/costly.
2. **Hard-to-value stocks:** Firms with uncertain cash flows (young, unprofitable) are most sensitive to sentiment.
3. **Sentiment composite:** IPO volume, first-day returns, closed-end fund discount, NYSE turnover, dividend premium.
4. **Historical episodes:** Nifty Fifty, Tech bubble — consistent with sentiment-driven mis-valuation.
5. **Top-down approach:** Macro sentiment measure explains cross-sectional return patterns.

## Key Quotes

> "The question is no longer whether investor sentiment affects stock prices, but rather how to measure investor sentiment and quantify its effects."

## Relevance to Thesis

- Conceptual underpinning for Reddit sentiment as a form of retail investor sentiment
- Limits-to-arbitrage justifies why noise-trading effects (H2's −32 bps) can persist
