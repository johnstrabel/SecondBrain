# Baker & Wurgler (2006) — Investor Sentiment and the Cross-Section of Stock Returns

**Full citation:** Baker, M. & Wurgler, J. (2006). Investor Sentiment and the Cross-Section of Stock Returns. *Journal of Finance*, 61(4), 1645–1680.

## Summary

Studies how investor sentiment affects the cross-section of stock returns. Argues that sentiment has larger effects on securities that are hard to value and hard to arbitrage. When beginning-of-period sentiment is low, subsequent returns are high for small, young, volatile, unprofitable, non-dividend-paying, and distressed stocks — and vice versa.

## Key Arguments

1. **Theory:** Stocks that are hardest to value (no earnings history, uncertain future) are most affected by sentiment swings; arbitrage is also most constrained for these stocks.
2. **Sentiment proxy:** Composite index from IPO volume, first-day returns, closed-end fund discount, NYSE turnover, dividend premium.
3. **Cross-sectional prediction:** High sentiment → low future returns for speculative stocks (demand was inflated); low sentiment → high future returns (oversold).
4. **Sign-flipping:** Several characteristics show sign-flipping predictive ability conditional on sentiment (unconditionally have no power).
5. **U-shape:** Both extreme growth and distressed stocks are sentiment-sensitive; stable middle firms are less affected.

## Key Quotes

> "When beginning-of-period proxies for sentiment are low, subsequent returns are relatively high for small stocks, young stocks, high volatility stocks, unprofitable stocks, non-dividend-paying stocks, extreme growth stocks, and distressed stocks."

## Relevance to Thesis

- Theoretical basis for H3 (cross-sectional heterogeneity in sentiment response)
- Beta, size, and volatility used as cross-sectional moderators in thesis directly motivated by this paper
- H3 not supported — thesis findings inconsistent with Baker & Wurgler's cross-sectional predictions at earnings-event horizon
