# Piotroski (2000) — Value Investing: The Use of Historical Financial Statement Information to Separate Winners from Losers

**Full citation:** Piotroski, J.D. (2000). Value Investing: The Use of Historical Financial Statement Information to Separate Winners from Losers. *Journal of Accounting Research*, 38 (Supplement), 1–41.

## Summary

Applies a simple accounting-based F-SCORE (9 binary signals across profitability, leverage/liquidity, and operating efficiency) to high book-to-market firms to distinguish future winners from losers. A strategy of buying high-F-SCORE and shorting low-F-SCORE firms among high BM stocks earns ~23% annual return 1976–1996. Results suggest market does not fully incorporate historical financial information into prices.

## Key Arguments

1. **F-SCORE construction:** 9 binary signals (positive ROA, positive cash flow, ROA improvement, accruals, leverage decrease, liquidity increase, no share issuance, gross margin improvement, asset turnover improvement) → 0–9 composite.
2. **Return distribution shift:** +7.5% mean annual return improvement for high-BM investors through F-SCORE screening; entire distribution shifts right.
3. **Winner-loser strategy:** Long top-F-SCORE + short bottom-F-SCORE = 23% annual return; robust across subsamples.
4. **Small cap effect:** Benefits concentrated in small and medium firms, low-turnover stocks, firms without analyst following.
5. **Market inefficiency:** Results imply market does not promptly impound past fundamental signals into prices (supports PEAD-type delayed reaction).

## Key Quotes

> "The market does not fully incorporate historical financial information into prices in a timely manner."
