# Cohen, Malloy & Nguyen (2020) — Lazy Prices

**Full citation:** Cohen, L., Malloy, C. & Nguyen, Q. (2020). Lazy Prices. *Journal of Finance*, 75(3), 1371–1415.

## Summary

Shows that changes to the language and construction of firms' annual (10-K) and quarterly (10-Q) financial reports have strong implications for future returns and operations. A portfolio that shorts "changers" (firms that altered their 10-K language significantly) and buys "nonchangers" earns up to 188 bps/month in alpha (>22% annually). No announcement effect — investors appear inattentive to these language changes.

## Key Arguments

1. **Text-change signal:** Similarity score between consecutive 10-Ks measures language change; large decreases signal future negative outcomes.
2. **Massive alpha:** Long nonchangers / short changers earns 188 bps/month; results robust to Fama-French factors.
3. **Inattention:** No announcement-day reaction — market learns slowly, implying true investor inattention to textual signals.
4. **Predictive scope:** Changes predict future earnings, profitability, news, and bankruptcies; the signal is economically broad.
5. **10-K growth:** Average 10-K now 6× longer than in 1995; number of changes has grown 12×, making processing harder for investors.

## Relevance to Thesis

- Evidence that text-based signals in financial filings predict returns — supports NLP-based approach in thesis
- Investor inattention to text mirrors the inattention mechanism behind PEAD (under-reaction)
- Relevant to the "information fusion" theme: SEC filings are another information layer beyond sentiment
- Cited in thesis Chapter 2 for the NLP/alternative data literature
