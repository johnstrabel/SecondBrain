# Sullivan, Timmermann & White (1999) — Data Snooping, Technical Trading Rule Performance, and the Bootstrap

**Full citation:** Sullivan, R., Timmermann, A. & White, H. (1999). Data Snooping, Technical Trading Rule Performance, and the Bootstrap. *Journal of Finance*, 54(5), 1647–1691.

## Summary

Applies White's (1997) "Reality Check" bootstrap methodology to evaluate technical trading rules while controlling for data-snooping bias. Expands Brock, Lakonishok & LeBaron's (1992) universe of 26 rules to a comprehensive set; applies to 100 years of daily DJIA data. After controlling for the full universe of tested rules, positive performance disappears.

## Key Arguments

1. **Data snooping definition:** When a dataset is used multiple times for model search, reported results overstate true performance; apparent anomalies may be spurious.
2. **Reality Check:** Tests whether the best rule in a universe of rules outperforms the benchmark after accounting for data snooping — controls false discovery rate across all rules.
3. **Main finding:** When the universe of rules is expanded and data snooping is controlled for, technical trading rules no longer show significant excess returns.
4. **Critique of Brock et al.:** Brock's 26 rules were a small subset; once the full universe is tested, the "best" rule's performance is unsurprising.
5. **Later sample:** Rules that worked in the historical DJIA sample do not persist out-of-sample (post-1986).

## Relevance to Thesis

- Key counterargument to Brock et al. in the technical analysis chapter
- Data-snooping concern also applies to the thesis's ML model (overfitting; AUC-ROC 0.520 may reflect genuine information, not data snooping)
- Motivates the thesis's use of a held-out test set (2021–2023) for ML evaluation
