## Lecture Content — Part 1: What Is a State and Why Does It Change?

### The State Under Pressure
- A state has: fixed territory, sovereignty, legal independence, legitimacy
- Globalisation and unmet demands (security, justice, economic growth) have weakened the state
- States increasingly cooperate to achieve goals they cannot reach alone
- Cooperation ranges from loose arrangements to deep integration

### Types of Political Organisation
| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| State | Free-standing entity with territory, sovereignty, independence, legitimacy |
| International organisation | Voluntary cooperation among states; intergovernmental in character |
| Regional integration association | Transfer, sharing or pooling of authority; intergovernmental *and* supranational |

**The EU is the world's most advanced example of a regional integration association.**

### Levels of Economic Integration (Balassa)
*(Diagram in slides — slide 17)*  
Free Trade Area → Customs Union → Common Market → Economic Union → Political Union

### Pros and Cons of Regional Integration

**Pros:**
- Reduces conflict; increases global influence; enlarges markets
- Promotes innovation; raises standards in weaker members
- Promotes democracy and human rights

**Cons:**
- Loss of sovereignty and national identity
- Democratic deficit in supranational institutions
- Risk of a distant European "superstate"
- Job losses from increased competition
- Financial burden of bailouts and transfers

## Lecture Content — Part 2: Theories of European Integration

**Central question:** Why and how do states voluntarily share or transfer sovereignty?

All major theories address three dimensions:
1. Who are the key actors?
2. What drives their preferences?
3. Where does integration ultimately lead?

*No single theory explains the EU fully — they are complementary lenses.*

### Federalism & Confederalism
- **Federalism:** States form a federation because they can no longer guarantee citizens' security alone; power divided between federal core and constituent units (normative theory)
- **Confederalism:** Members retain sovereignty, delegate only limited powers to a centre
- **EU confederal traits:** Separate national legal systems, independent tax systems, no common military, primary national loyalty
- Historical examples: USA (1781–1788), Germany (1815–1871)

### Neo-Functionalism & Intergovernmentalism
- **Neo-functionalism (Haas, 1958):**
  - Cooperation in one sector spills over into others → integration becomes self-sustaining
  - Political actors shift loyalties toward a new supranational centre
  - Fell out of favour after de Gaulle's Empty Chair Crisis (1965–66) — showed spill-over is not automatic
  - Haas declared theory "obsolescent" in 1975; partially revived by Schmitter, Sandholtz, Stone Sweet
- **Intergovernmentalism (Hoffmann, 1966):**
  - Member state governments are the key actors; not supranational institutions
  - Integration driven by national interests and interstate bargaining
  - Integration occurs only when gains permanently outweigh losses for nation-states

### Liberal Intergovernmentalism & Multi-Level Governance
- **Liberal intergovernmentalism (Moravcsik, 1998):**
  - Three stages: national preference formation (domestic economic interest groups) → interstate bargaining → institutional choice (credible commitments)
  - Explains major milestones well (Single European Act, Maastricht); less suited to incremental day-to-day EU decision-making
- **Multi-level governance (Marks & Hooghe, 2001):**
  - Authority dispersed both upward (to supranational institutions) and downward (to regional/local governments) from central states
  - Both vertical and horizontal relationships matter
  - Developed in response to Maastricht Treaty (1992); challenges state-centric models

### What Can These Theories Explain?

| Event | Best-fit theory |
|---|---|
| Brexit | Intergovernmentalism ✓ (states can reassert sovereignty); neo-functionalism ✗ (couldn't explain reversal) |
| Eurozone crisis | Liberal intergovernmentalism ✓ (bailout logic); multi-level governance ✓ (implementation failures) |
| Day-to-day EU governance | Multi-level governance + institutionalism (not grand bargain theories) |

**The EU today sits between economic union and political union.**
