## Static vs. Dynamic Effects in Balassa/Sapir

### Static Effects (one-time, allocation-level)

These come purely from removing barriers — no structural change required:

- **Trade creation**: Domestic high-cost production is replaced by cheaper imports from partner countries once tariffs fall. Resources reallocate to where each country has comparative advantage → net welfare gain.
- **Trade diversion**: The common external tariff redirects imports away from cheaper non-member countries toward more expensive member-country producers. This is a **loss** — you're buying from a less efficient source purely because the tariff makes outside imports artificially expensive.

The net static effect depends on which dominates. For the EU, trade creation historically outweighed diversion, but it's not guaranteed.

---

### Dynamic Effects (cumulative, structural — "the three effects")

These are ongoing and compound over time, which is why Balassa argued they matter more:

**1. Economies of scale**  
Protected national markets were too small to let firms reach minimum efficient scale. A common market of 300M+ people lets firms expand output and drive down average costs. This is especially powerful for industries with high fixed costs (aerospace, pharma, autos).

**2. Competition effect**  
Firms previously sheltered behind tariffs now face European competitors. This forces down prices, reduces X-inefficiency (slack inside firms), and speeds up exit of uncompetitive producers. Consumers gain directly.

**3. Investment/FDI effect**  
Integration signals stability and a large protected market, attracting both intra-EU investment and FDI from outside (US, Japanese firms building inside the EU to access it). Capital deepening raises productivity over time.

---

### Location Effects (static and dynamic)

This is where the spatial/geographic dimension comes in — and Sapir incorporates New Economic Geography (Krugman) which Balassa couldn't have:

**Static location effects**: Based on factor endowments (Heckscher-Ohlin). Countries specialize in goods using their abundant factors — labor-intensive production shifts east (Poland, Hungary), capital-intensive stays west. This is efficient but creates losers in the short run (workers in industries that lose protection).

**Dynamic location effects**: Agglomeration. Once scale economies and lower trade costs interact, firms cluster — they want to be near suppliers, customers, and skilled labor pools. This creates a **core-periphery risk**: activity concentrates in the already-rich core (Germany, Benelux, northern France), leaving the periphery behind. The EU Structural Funds exist precisely to offset this.

---

### Drawbacks Sapir Highlights

|Drawback|Nature|
|---|---|
|Trade diversion|Static cost; permanent if external tariffs stay high|
|Asymmetric shocks in EMU|Monetary union without fiscal union; Germany and Greece can't both have the "right" interest rate|
|Agglomeration divergence|Dynamic location effect works against poor regions unless offset by transfers|
|Short-run adjustment costs|Industries losing protection shed workers before scale/competition gains materialize|
|"Race to the bottom" risk|Tax and regulatory competition as countries attract mobile capital|

**The 2010 Eurozone crisis** is Sapir's main exhibit: Balassa himself warned that monetary union without fiscal union would be unstable. The static and dynamic gains from the common market were real, but the institutional design of EMU was flawed — no common budget, no transfer mechanism, no lender of last resort.

---

### Short vs. Long Run

The tension Sapir emphasizes:

- **Short run**: Trade diversion and adjustment costs dominate. Workers lose jobs in protected sectors. Firms in weak regions face competition they can't handle yet. Fiscal strain.
- **Long run**: Dynamic effects compound. Scale economies lower costs, competition raises productivity, investment flows in. Empirically, EU integration is credited with 5–10% GDP gain over 40 years for member states — but this is unevenly distributed.

The core insight is that **static effects are a one-time level shift** (you move to a better allocation once and stop). **Dynamic effects are rate-of-growth effects** — they keep compounding, which is why Balassa was right to argue they are the dominant justification for deep integration over a long horizon.
