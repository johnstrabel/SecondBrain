## Lecture Content — EU Integration Theory

### Balassa Stages of Integration (recap from Session 1)
1. Free Trade Area
2. Customs Union
3. Common Market (four freedoms: goods, services, capital, labour)
4. Economic Union (common policies)
5. Political Union (not yet achieved)

Current EU status: **Economic/Monetary Union in progress** — not a federal state. ECB handles eurozone monetary policy; national central banks remain for non-euro members (Czech koruna etc.)

### Three Welfare Effects of Integration (Sapir Chapter 3)

**Static effects** ([[Sapir (2011)]] Chapter 3):
- *Trade creation* — domestic production replaced by cheaper partner imports; net welfare gain
- *Trade diversion* — common external tariff diverts from cheap non-members to pricier EU partners; net welfare loss
- Net depends on relative magnitudes; EU historically net positive on trade creation

**Dynamic effects — the three mechanisms:**
1. *Economies of scale* — common market allows firms to reach minimum efficient scale impossible in small protected national markets
2. *Competition* — EU rivals force down prices and X-inefficiency; consumers gain
3. *Investment/FDI* — stable large market attracts capital inflows; productivity growth

**Location effects:**
- *Static:* Heckscher-Ohlin; labour-abundant countries (Eastern Europe) attract labour-intensive production; capital stays west
- *Dynamic:* Agglomeration (Krugman [[New Economic Geography]]); core-periphery concentration; EU Structural Funds as corrective

**Short vs. long run:** Short run = adjustment costs + trade diversion dominate; long run = dynamic effects compound (5–10% GDP attributed to 40 years of integration). See [[Tracking/Learning Log/2026-04-21]] for connections to thesis.

## Part 2 — EU Institutions (Transcript Pt.2, 16:07)

### Summary
Covered the core EU institutional architecture and how it produces legislation. Three main bodies — European Commission (executive), Council of the EU (ministers), European Parliament (directly elected) — negotiate all secondary legislation through a process called the **trialogue**. The European Council (heads of government) sits above these as a strategic direction-setter but does not legislate. Also covered qualified majority voting, regulations vs. directives, the ETS as a case study in EU policymaking, the 28th Regime proposal, and the ECB's primary mandate.

---

### EU Institutional Architecture

#### European Council
- **Who:** Heads of government of all member states + its own President
- **President:** Antonio Costa (current)
- **Role:** Sets the EU's strategic priorities — foreign policy, budget frameworks, major crisis responses (e.g. sanctions on Russia, migration strategy). Does **not** co-legislate.
- **Format:** Summits; consensus-based
- *This is the institution simulated in the EUCO class exercise*

#### European Commission
- **Who:** 27 Commissioners (one per country) + President
- **President:** Ursula von der Leyen — second mandate (2024–2029); two mandates maximum
- **Role:** Sole right to **propose** legislation; guardian of the treaties; ensures compliance; manages EU budget execution
- **Structure:** 27 Directorates-General covering all policy areas; also specialised agencies (e.g. European Defence Agency, Eurostat)
- Commissioners do **not** represent national interests — they represent the EU as a whole
- Communicates proposals early via **green papers** (consultation) and **white papers** (policy intent) before formal legislative proposals

#### Council of the EU
- **Who:** Ministers from each member state (composition changes by topic — energy ministers for energy, finance ministers for finance, etc.)
- **Role:** Co-legislates with the European Parliament on all Commission proposals; approves the EU budget together with Parliament
- **Presidency:** Rotates every 6 months by country (Czech Republic held it twice during energy crises — 2009 gas dispute and recent energy crisis)
- **Voting:** Most legislation requires **Qualified Majority Voting (QMV)** — majority of states *and* ≥65% of EU population represented. Some areas require unanimity.

#### European Parliament
- **Who:** 720+ MEPs directly elected by citizens every 5 years
- **Seats:** Max 96 per country (Germany 96, Czech Republic 21)
- **Role:** Co-legislates with Council of the EU; approves budget; votes on Commission President
- **Structure:** MEPs sit in **European fractions** (cross-national groupings), not national party delegations. Largest fraction after 2024 elections: European People's Party (~24%)
- **Voter turnout problem:** Czech EP turnout ~18–28%; most Eastern European countries similarly low. Citizens perceive EP as distant; parties under-campaign for EP elections.
- Only institution where citizens have a **direct vote**

---

### How EU Legislation Works

**Trialogue** — the three-way negotiation between Commission, Council of the EU, and Parliament that shapes all secondary legislation:

```
Commission proposes → Council of the EU + Parliament negotiate → law agreed
```

**Types of secondary legislation:**
| Type | How it works |
|---|---|
| **Regulation** | Directly applicable in all member states as written; no national transposition; binding exactly as passed |
| **Directive** | Sets a goal (e.g. reduce emissions by X%); member states choose *how* to achieve it; can be amended during transposition |
| **Decision** | Binding on specific addressees only |

### Case Study: EU Emissions Trading System (ETS)

- Green paper published 2000 → system launched 2005 (5-year lead time for businesses to prepare)
- EU was global front-runner on carbon pricing; still one of very few countries with meaningful carbon price
- **ETS 2.0** now expanding to transport and households
- Post-COVID and energy crisis: EU legislative pace has accelerated significantly — less lead time than the ETS example

### 28th Regime (Fresh Proposal, March 2026)

- Commission proposal to create a **common EU business law framework** as an optional 28th legal regime alongside the 27 existing national ones
- Companies could choose to operate under this single EU framework instead of a national one
- Still in very early negotiation phase
- **Legislative Train Schedule** (publicly available at EC website) tracks all proposals in real time — useful research tool for debate prep
- *This is one of the available debate topics*

---

### European Central Bank (ECB)

- **Primary mandate:** Price stability — inflation target ~2%
- Countries joining the Eurozone surrender independent monetary policy
- **Monetary trilemma:** Cannot simultaneously have (1) fixed exchange rate, (2) free capital movement, and (3) independent monetary policy — joining the Eurozone means choosing (1) and (2), giving up (3)
- Czech Republic retains the koruna and its own central bank (not in Eurozone)

### Key Distinctions to Remember for Exam

| Term | Don't confuse with |
|---|---|
| **European Council** (heads of government, strategic) | **Council of the EU** (ministers, legislative) |
| **Regulation** (directly binding, no transposition) | **Directive** (goal-setting, flexible transposition) |
| **QMV** (majority of states + 65% population) | **Unanimity** (required for treaties, sensitive areas) |
| **Commission proposes** | Parliament + Council of EU dispose (negotiate and approve) |
