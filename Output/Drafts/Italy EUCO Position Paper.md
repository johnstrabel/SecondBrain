# European Council Simulation — Position Summary
## Italy | Migration and Refugee Policy
**Representing:** The Italian Prime Minister  
**Submitted by:** John Strabel

---

## Part 1: Summary of Italy's Position

Italy occupies a structurally unique and acutely vulnerable position in the European migration debate. As one of the EU's primary entry points — alongside Greece — Italy bears a disproportionate share of the physical burden of irregular migration arriving from across the Mediterranean. Daily arrivals from North and Sub-Saharan Africa, predominantly transiting through Libya, have pushed Italian reception centers beyond capacity. The Italian position is therefore not ideological but operational: the current distribution of responsibility is unsustainable and structurally unfair to entry-point states.

Italy's core demands at this European Council are:

**Burden-sharing.** Italy's most urgent priority is a binding, mandatory mechanism for relocating migrants currently held in Italian facilities to other EU member states. The principle that entry-point countries bear sole responsibility for processing and hosting all arrivals — enshrined in the Dublin Regulation — is, in Italy's view, incompatible with both the scale of current flows and the spirit of European solidarity. Italy will push strongly for a permanent, automatic relocation system rather than voluntary pledges, which have historically been unfulfilled.

**Mediterranean naval operations.** Italy calls for expanded EU naval presence in the Mediterranean and Adriatic, both to rescue migrants at sea and to interdict and prosecute people smugglers operating out of Libya. Italy seeks formal burden-sharing on Search and Rescue (SAR) costs, which it currently absorbs disproportionately, and requests increased Frontex assets — vessels, personnel, and surveillance capacity — deployed to Italian territorial waters.

**Screening and border management.** Italy supports robust "hotspot" processing centers at EU external borders, provided these are EU-funded and EU-staffed rather than solely the responsibility of the entry-point state. Frontex and EASO (now EUAA) should lead screening operations, with all arrivals fingerprinted and registered in the Schengen Information System (SIS) before any onward movement. Rejected asylum seekers must be returned effectively — Italy demands enforceable EU-wide readmission agreements with origin and transit countries.

**External dimension.** Italy views addressing migration at its source as essential. It supports substantial EU financial assistance to refugees in Turkey, Lebanon, and Jordan; targeted development and stabilisation investment in key African origin countries; and EU engagement to establish protected zones in Syria. Of particular strategic importance is Libya: Italy considers Libyan instability the single largest driver of Mediterranean flows and calls for a coordinated EU diplomatic and security engagement with Libyan authorities on migration cooperation.

**Schengen integrity.** Italy supports preserving free movement within the Schengen Area but insists on strengthened external border controls and real-time information sharing on the movement of asylum seekers — including those whose applications have been rejected — across member states.

## Part 2: Commentary on Italy's Approach

Italy's position is internally coherent and grounded in genuine structural grievance. The Dublin Regulation's "first-entry" principle was designed for a different era of migration; applied to Italy in the current context, it produces outcomes that are both practically unworkable and politically explosive. Italy is right to identify this as the root institutional failure, and its push for mandatory burden-sharing reflects a principle that most EU member states privately acknowledge as correct, even if they resist it publicly.

However, Italy's approach carries significant strategic tensions.

**The tension between solidarity and deterrence.** Italy's simultaneous demands for burden-sharing (a more welcoming signal) and robust returns, tighter screening, and Libyan border cooperation (a more restrictive signal) are difficult to reconcile into a single coherent message. Partner states — particularly Northern European countries — will question whether Italy's real goal is managing migration more humanely within Europe, or reducing arrivals regardless of the means. Italy risks being seen as wanting other countries to host migrants it has already accepted, while simultaneously pursuing policies designed to ensure fewer migrants arrive in the first place. This is a negotiating position rather than a principled one, and sophisticated counterparts will probe it.

**The Libya question.** Italy's reliance on Libyan cooperation as a migration management tool is its most vulnerable flank. Agreements with Libyan authorities — whoever currently holds authority in a fragmented state — have been repeatedly criticised by human rights organisations for facilitating detention conditions that violate EU fundamental rights standards. Italy will face pressure from Northern and Western member states to either defend this approach or distance itself from it. A defensible response involves framing Libya cooperation as a transitional necessity while advocating for EU-level, rights-compliant engagement to replace bilateral Italian deals.

**Coalition dynamics.** Italy's natural allies in this negotiation are the other Mediterranean entry-point states — Greece, Spain, Cyprus, Malta — who share the structural grievance. Together they represent a credible blocking coalition on any conclusions that entrench the status quo. Italy should also cultivate support from member states with strong humanitarian traditions (Germany, Sweden, Netherlands) on the external dimension and resettlement questions, where common ground exists, while accepting that Visegrád states (Poland, Hungary, Czechia, Slovakia) will resist any mandatory relocation mechanism.

**Overall assessment.** Italy's position is reactive rather than visionary — shaped by immediate operational pressure rather than a long-term EU migration architecture. This is understandable given the scale of the challenge, but it leaves Italy without a compelling positive offer to the Council. A stronger Italian position would pair the burden-sharing demand with a concrete proposal for a permanent EU asylum processing and relocation mechanism, giving partner states something to agree to rather than merely something to resist.
